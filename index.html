Budgeted Cross-Lingual Reasoning: Token Efficiency, Language Compliance, and Serving-Tier Instability in Open-Weight Reasoning Models

Preprint in preparation, 2026

Reasoning traces, not answers, dominate the cost of deploying reasoning models. This study asks whether instructing a model to think in a different language can buy the same accuracy for fewer tokens. I evaluated 4 open-weight reasoning models across 5 instructed reasoning languages on BIG-Bench Extra Hard, under per-model token budgets, with a staged eligibility gate that screened 8 model families.

  • Language compliance collapses on hard tasks. Directives that achieve near-perfect in-language reasoning on standard benchmarks stop working on hard items. Three of four models revert to English no matter what you ask for.
  • Scoring policy, not capability, decides the ranking. Whether truncated responses count as failures single-handedly determines which model wins. Both readings are true, and they answer different questions.
  • The serving infrastructure is a confounder. A provider's discounted batch tier destabilized large mixture-of-experts models at temperature 0. Rerunning every batch-truncated call synchronously completed 62 percent of them, enough to dissolve a model ranking that had looked statistically significant.

trAId

Text a photo of anything you want to sell. trAId identifies the item, researches comparable eBay listings with a real logged-in browser, prices it, drafts the listing, gets your approval over text, publishes, then negotiates with buyers autonomously. Built in 24 hours at CalHacks 2026.

The interesting parts: a durable workflow that pauses for human approval and survives restarts, one persistent authenticated browser context, and negotiation guardrails enforced in code rather than in the prompt.
